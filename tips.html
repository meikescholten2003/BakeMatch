Cooking Techniques

Here you'll find all the cooking techniques, from A to Z. Each cooking technique is explained with a concise explanation. A handy guide with all the essential cooking terms, such as blanching, braising, and gratinating.

Al dente

The point at which food, usually vegetables, is cooked and tender, but still offers a slight resistance when bitten into. This preserves the texture and nutritional value of the food.

Amuse-bouche

An amuse-bouche is a small, flavorful dish, usually served before a meal to whet the appetite. It is intended as a culinary surprise and is often served in one or two bites. Amuse-bouches often feature unique flavor combinations and ingredients to surprise and delight guests before the main course is served.

Bain-marie

A bain-marie is a technique in which food is heated by placing it in a bowl or pan that sits in a larger pan of hot water. It is often used to melt chocolate or sauces, for example, without direct contact with the hot source, allowing it to heat evenly and gently. This preserves the quality of the chocolate and prevents it from burning or lumping.

Bisque

A bisque is a creamy soup usually made from shellfish, such as lobster, shrimp, or crab. Its distinguishing feature is that the shells and heads of the shellfish are used to create a rich, fragrant broth before being mixed with cream and spices to finish the soup. The result is a flavorful soup.

Breading

Breading is the process of coating food first with flour, then with beaten egg, and finally with breadcrumbs or breadcrumbs. This creates a crispy outer layer when baked or fried.

Blanching

Blanching is a cooking technique in which food is briefly placed in boiling water and then immediately cooled in cold water. It is often used to prepare vegetables and preserve their color, texture and flavor before further processing them into other dishes.

Braising

A cooking method in which food is first seared and then slowly cooked in a closed pot with a small amount of liquid. This results in tender, juicy food and concentrated flavors. You can braise meat, fish, poultry, and vegetables.

Broiling

Cooking food in a hot oven or pan with oil to create a crispy exterior and retain juices within. This is often used for meat, poultry, and vegetables.

Caramelization

A process in which sugar is heated until it melts and turns brown, creating a deep, rich caramel flavor. Caramelization is often used for desserts such as crème brûlée and sauces like caramel sauce. Be careful not to burn the sugar, as this can happen very quickly.

Confit

A preservation technique in which food, such as fruit or meat, is slowly cooked in fat or sugar water to preserve and soften it. This process helps preserve food and develop rich flavors.

Curling

The process by which liquid and solids in a dish separate, often occurring with sauces that have been cooked too hot. Curdling can occur when fat and water are not properly emulsified.

Deep-frying

A cooking technique in which food is submerged in hot oil, giving it a crispy exterior. This is often used for fried snacks such as fries, chicken nuggets, and donuts.

Deglazing

A cooking technique in which liquid, usually wine, stock, vinegar, or water, is added to a hot pan to loosen and combine any browned or flavorful residue that has formed on the bottom, resulting in a concentrated and flavorful sauce. This process is often used to enhance the flavors of a dish and thicken the sauce.

Filling

A cutting technique in which fish or meat is cut from the bone or bone. This allows you to remove the bone or bone and divide the meat into neat pieces. You can do this with fish, poultry, and game, among other things.

Flambéing

A cooking technique in which alcohol is poured over a dish and then ignited to create a flaming effect and add extra flavor. This is often used for desserts like crêpes suzette and meat dishes like steak.

Gratin

A dish, usually made with potatoes or vegetables, covered with a layer of cheese, breadcrumbs, or breadcrumbs and then gratinated (baked) in the oven, creating a crispy crust. You can do this using the broiler setting on your oven, for example, or with a blowtorch. The word "gratin" refers to the golden brown crust. See our potato gratin recipe here.

Gratinating

A technique in which food is baked in the oven to form a golden brown crust, usually with cheese or breadcrumbs. This gives dishes a crispy and flavorful topping.

Julienne

A cutting technique in which food, usually vegetables, is cut into thin strips, similar to matchsticks. Julienne-cut vegetables are often used as a garnish in salads and soups. A very well-known example is carrot julienne.

Marination

The soaking of food in a mixture of, for example, oil, vinegar, herbs, and spices to add flavor and soften it. Marinating enhances the flavor and tenderness of meat, fish, and vegetables.

Whipping

The process of vigorously whipping ingredients, usually egg whites or cream, with a whisk or mixer to trap air bubbles and create a light and airy texture. This is often done when making meringues, whipped cream, and cakes.

Poaching

A cooking technique in which food is gently cooked in a liquid, usually water or broth, without bubbling. Poaching is often used for preparing eggs, fish, and poultry because it produces a tender and even cooking. It preserves flavor well, and no fat is needed to cook the food.

Puree

Food, usually vegetables, fruit, or potatoes, that has been mashed to a smooth, creamy consistency, often seasoned and flavored. Puree can be served as a side dish or as a base for soups and sauces. For example, you can make your own mashed potatoes with potatoes and milk.

Reduce

The process of reducing a liquid by simmering it, concentrating the flavors and thickening the sauce. This is often done to thicken and enhance sauces.

Rolling Ground Meat

The process of frying ground meat in a pan until it is browned and cooked through. While frying, you break the ground meat apart. This is often done when making dishes such as pasta sauce, chili con carne, tacos, and casseroles.

Roux

A mixture of melted butter and flour used as a base for thickening sauces and soups. The roux is first cooked until it reaches the desired color before other ingredients are added.

Sautéing

A cooking method in which food is quickly sautéed in a hot pan with a small amount of oil or butter. This ensures fast cooking and preserves the flavor and texture of the food. It is often used for meat, vegetables, and shrimp.

Sautéing an Onion

Sautéing an onion is the process of frying chopped onions in oil or butter until they are soft and golden brown to bring out their sweet flavor. This is a common step in preparing stews, soups, and sauces.

Simmering

A slow cooking method in which food is cooked over low heat in a covered pot, usually with liquid. Simmering ensures slow and even cooking, making meats and stews flavorful and tender.

Soaking

A cooking method in which food is briefly placed in hot water to prepare it for further cooking. Soaking is often used to soften hard vegetables or loosen the skins of tomatoes, for example. Raisins should also be washed and soaked before using. This makes them softer and juicier.

Sourdough

A type of bread made using a natural fermentation method that uses wild yeast and bacteria to make the dough rise, resulting in a distinct flavor and texture. Sourdough bread has a slightly sour taste and a longer shelf life than regular bread. No baker's yeast is added to sourdough bread. It requires a longer rising time than regular bread.

Sous-vide

Sous-vide, also known as vacuum cooking, is a cooking method in which food is sealed in an airtight bag and cooked slowly and evenly in a water bath at a precise temperature, usually lower than normal. This results in juicy and tender meat or other dishes because the ingredients do not dry out during cooking.

Steaming

A cooking technique in which food is prepared by placing it over a boiling liquid so that the rising steam cooks the food. This is a healthy method because it preserves nutrients. It also creates a juicy texture.

Stir-frying

A quick cooking method in which food is constantly stirred in a pan over high heat, usually with oil, soy sauce, and vegetables. Stir-frying is popular in Asian cuisine and preserves the texture and crunch of vegetables. A wok is often used for this, and it's also called stir-frying.

Thickening (Soup/Sauce Thickening)

Adding a thickening agent, such as flour, cornstarch, or potato starch, to a liquid to thicken its consistency and create sauces or soups. This creates a thick, creamy texture.

Zest

The grated peel of citrus fruits is used to add aroma and flavor to dishes. Zest can be added to desserts, sauces, and cocktails for a fresh citrus kick.
